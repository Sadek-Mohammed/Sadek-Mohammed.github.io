Grade 11 Second Semester Capstone Project
Capstone Project is a per-semester project, done at my school to solve one of Egypt's 11 Grand Challenges. In that project, a portfolio (in the form of a research paper) is written, a fully-working hardware prototype is built, and a poster is deisgned for the Exhibition (a 10-min presentation in front of Egyptian University Professors). The projects contributes to 60% of the total grade of each and every subject in my school during grades 10 and 11, which gives the project a special significance as one of the main grading criteria in the school's system.

Egypt's 11 Grand Challenges

Work to eradicate public health issues/diseases

Improve sources of clean water

Egypt's water resources are mainly used for irrigation and agricultural purposes (over 77%), and that consumption is almost-completely dependent on the River Nile which provides Egypt with 55.5 billion cubic meters of Freshwater, representing about 75% of Egypt's water resources. That daunting dependence on the River Nile encourages further research on other reliable resources of clean water.

Increase industrial base for Egypt

In Egypt, 89% of the industrial processes are located in Cairo and some northern governorates, which relates to how poor is industry in the other parts of Egypt. Furthermore, manufacturing output represented only 20% of Egypt's Gross Domestic Product, employing 20% of the active labor power. The last thing to consider is the quality of products, and as the social life in Egypt is getting worse, the quality of products decreases daily.

Reduce Urban Congestion

Egypt's population is reasonble relative to its area; however, the real problem is with its distribution. More than fifth of Egypt's population (19 million inhabitants) live in Cairo, Egypt's biggest city, with a density that ranges from 40,000 people in each square kilometer to 100,000 people in older districts.

Improve use of arid areas

The untolerable population density in Cairo encourages further research on how to distribute Egypt's citizens in population-light places like The Western Desert, The Eastern Desert, and Sinai Peninsula.

Improve the use of alternative energies

Egypt is the largest non-OPEC oil producer in Africa, so 95% of the population has access to Africa; however, the per-capita consumption is too much. In the period from 2003 to 2014, the per-capita electricity consumption increased from 1128.721 kWh to 1657.769 kWh, and the percentage is still increasing with the increased use of technological devices.

Deal with the exponential population growth

Egypt's population increases by about 1.5 million people each year, which is an extremely unacceptable rate as Egypt's population will reach 160 million people by 2050. Another problem is that the biggest portion of the population is of age less than or equal to 9 years old, persuading reaching a fully-working solution.

Increase Opporunities for Egyptians to stay and work in Egypt.

Reduce Pollution

Due to a dramatic increase in Egypt's population, Egypt's total amount of Greenhouse Gases (GHGs) increased from about 130 Metric Tons of CO2 Equivalent (Mt. CO2) to about 320 Mt. CO2 from 1990 to 2016. This economic change is accompanied with decreases of Agriculture, Health Expenditures, and Labour Productivity in Egypt, encouraging further research.

Recycle and Retain garbage for recycling

Egypt produces about 60 million tons of solid wastes per year; however, only a small fraction of all of that is properly recycled. Conflicts with the Egyptian Governement, some companies, and local garbage collectiors made the companies obliged to recycle 20% of the waste which far less than the waste collectors whose methods of waste managing are lacking.
